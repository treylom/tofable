# Air Layering: Propagating Woody Stems Without Cutting Them Off First

_Source: clipped from a plant-care newsletter, saved for the notes vault._

Most propagation guides start with "take a cutting" — but for thick,
woody stems (rubber trees, fiddle-leaf figs, dracaenas), cutting first and
rooting after can mean weeks of a bare cutting sitting in water with no
guarantee it takes. Air layering flips the order: you root the stem
*while it's still attached to the parent plant*, and only cut it free once
roots have already formed.

## How it works

1. Pick a stem section at least pencil-thickness, a few inches below a
   node.
2. Make a small upward-angled cut into the stem (about a third of the way
   through) or strip a narrow ring of bark all the way around.
3. Dust the wound with rooting hormone if you have it — not required, but
   it speeds things up.
4. Pack damp sphagnum moss around the wound, about the size of a tennis
   ball.
5. Wrap the moss in plastic wrap and tie it off above and below like a
   sealed pouch, so it holds moisture without drying out or rotting.
6. Wait. Depending on the plant, roots show through the moss anywhere from
   3 to 10 weeks.
7. Once you can see a healthy web of roots filling the moss ball, cut the
   stem just below the new root ball and pot it up as its own plant.

## Why bother, compared to a regular cutting

- The parent plant keeps feeding the site the whole time, so the odds of
  the new plant surviving the transition are much higher than a bare
  cutting.
- Works on stems that are too thick or too woody to root reliably in water
  or soil alone.
- You end up with an already-rooted, healthy start rather than a bare
  stick you're hoping takes.

## Watch out for

- Letting the moss dry out — check it every week or two and re-wet if it
  feels dry through the plastic.
- Wrapping too tightly and cutting off circulation to the rest of the
  plant above the wound.
- Cutting the new plant free too early, before there's a real root system
  — give it the full weeks, not just "some roots visible."
